Clinical Evidence Management System

Module 5 — Evidence Integration Procedure

After a source has been cited, the writer still has to show readers how that evidence supports the claim being made.

Evidence is not fully doing its job when it is simply dropped into a paragraph. Readers need help understanding how the finding, quotation, or paraphrase connects to the reasoning.

Integration is functional, not decorative. A citation may be present and still leave the paragraph weak if the evidence is disconnected, unexplained, or hard to follow.

In this module, you will review brief writing situations and determine what aspect of evidence integration is most disrupted.

What Evidence Integration Does

Readers need more than a claim followed by a source. They need explanation that shows what the evidence means and why it matters in the paragraph.

Integration Check

Use this quick rule during the cases.

  1. Does the evidence clearly connect to the claim?
  2. Does the paragraph explain how the evidence supports the reasoning?
  3. Can the reader follow the evidence without confusion or gaps?
🔗

Claim–Evidence Connection

The reader should be able to see why this evidence belongs with this claim.

  • what is the paragraph arguing?
  • how does the evidence fit?
  • is the support actually relevant?
Reminder: Relevance is not enough; the paragraph needs a visible connection.
🧠

Reasoning

Evidence should be explained, not left to “speak for itself.”

  • what does the evidence show?
  • why does it matter here?
  • how does it support the point?
Reminder: Explanation is what turns evidence into support.
🧩

Avoiding Disconnection

A source can be present and still feel dropped, detached, or unfinished.

  • dropped quotations
  • unexplained findings
  • citation without integration
Reminder: Evidence should not stand alone without interpretation.
🧭

Reader Clarity

The paragraph should help the reader follow the movement from claim to evidence to explanation.

  • clear sequence
  • coherent paragraph movement
  • easy-to-follow reasoning
Reminder: Clear integration reduces confusion and strengthens credibility.
🏥

Professional Use of Evidence

In healthcare writing, unsupported or poorly integrated evidence weakens communication and decision-making.

  • evidence-based recommendations
  • clear justification
  • professional accountability
Reminder: Readers need usable reasoning, not just source presence.
Key Principle

Evidence should be integrated into reasoning, not simply attached to a claim. Strong paragraphs help readers see how the evidence supports the point being made.

Evidence Integration Note

Explain why evidence should be integrated into reasoning rather than simply attached to a claim in healthcare writing.

Documentation Note
Model Documentation Statement

Evidence must be integrated into reasoning so that readers can clearly see how it supports the claim being made. Simply attaching a source or citation is not enough, because evidence that is left unexplained may seem disconnected or incomplete. In healthcare writing, integration matters because readers need coherent reasoning that shows what the evidence demonstrates, how it supports the point, and why the recommendation or conclusion is justified.

Successful Integration Example
“Sleep quality should be assessed during inpatient recovery. Patients with interrupted sleep often report slower recovery and lower satisfaction (Morris et al., 2020), which suggests that improving sleep conditions may support better patient outcomes.”
  • Claim: The paragraph establishes a clear point about assessing sleep quality.
  • Evidence: The study finding gives relevant support connected to recovery.
  • Explanation: “which suggests” explains what the evidence means in this paragraph.
  • Coherence: The reader can follow the movement from claim to evidence to conclusion without a gap.
Transfer Forward

When writing: Move from claim to evidence to explanation so the reader can see what the source shows, how it supports the point, and why it matters in the paragraph.
